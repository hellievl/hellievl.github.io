Daisy Miller, Summer Fun

Trying to get back in the habit of blogging. Here are some quick thoughts on one movie I watched and one book I finished this week.

Daisy Miller (1974): Swung by Annie Belle's on Wednesday "after work" (still feels so weird to say). We ended up watching Pete Bogdanovich's Daisy Miller adaptation*. I expected it to be kind of hokey and bad but it was really truly incredible. The dialogue, characteristically dense and witty, was delivered at one million miles per hour like it was a screwball comedy straight out of the 40s. The whole film is so frenetic, electric: Winterbourne speed walking around the castle, forcing out the most anxious laughter you've ever heard as Daisy flits around and treats him like her little pet dog; Randolph pulling his fucking teeth out of his pocket. The looks. The looks! Furtive glance fans (me) were eating start to finish. So much histrionic desire contained behind a pair of eyes, a dam threatening to crack at any moment. Kind of a heterosexual, conflict avoidant The Music Lovers. Unfortunately, a couple missteps stop this from being a true Hellie all-timer. The scene where Winterbourne is informed of Daisy's death is perfect. The camera stays parked outside the front door of the hotel, the viewer is peering in as the proprietor breaks the news. We watch his smile fall from his face, we hear nothing. "What a genius way to end this amazing movie" I think. WRONG. It cuts to the actual ending scene at her funeral, which is played pretty straight and has a fair amount of inconsequential dialogue. Imagine if we had just been left standing outside that hotel, looking in. It would have been so perfect...if I had been in the editor's booth that day things would have gone down a little differently. Honestly that's the only complaint I can remember at this juncture. I guess it drags in a few spots, but nothing I can't forgive, especially when Pete's giving us so many freaky camera angles to watch the drama unfold.

Summer Fun: This took me like a month to read. Everyone told me it was the Great Transsexual American Novel, but I thought it was kind of boring. Extremely overwritten, so much unearned detail! I understand that the whole book is built on Beach Boys references, but I can only be reminded that Mike Love is evil and sucks so many times before it starts to feel like it's just fandom style winks and nods without any narrative significance. Every sentence felt like it was trying extremely hard to be profound and impactful, which just meant that the really good stuff got lost in the mix ("Someday, marriage will happen to me." goes crazy). I don't mean to be too bitchy. There is a really excellent book buried inside this one. The last 50 pages are leaps and bounds better than the entire book preceding them, mostly due to the fact that we drop a lot of the uninteresting baggage (Caroline). It starts moving along a little quicker, less baroque. A book like this cannot meander. A book like this needs to run directly towards a truth as quickly and desperately as it can, like pop music. Whether it reaches that truth is beside the point.

*Funny story about Daisy Miller. Like a week before I met Annie Belle Loren loaned me a copy, which she had read on Annie Belle's recommendation. During one of our very first conversations Annie Belle brought up James and I mentioned that I'd been reading it. This both caused us to realize Loren was our mutual close friend and that I was basically reading Daisy Miller on her recommendation by proxy. Point is she was giving me homework before I even met her.
