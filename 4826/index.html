Three Long Movies

Va Savoir (3/31): Loren, Joan, and I all piled into Joan's car (whose make and model escape me, white hatchback) to drive down to Doc. I knew nothing about what I was about to watch aside from the fact that is was part of their "Late Rivette" series. I had only seen Le Pont du Nord prior to this, but I loved it so much I was totally willing to give it a shot without any further investigation. Anyway we hit some traffic, and ended up missing the first 20 minutes or so. Even still this ended up being one of the most exciting moviegoing experiences of my year so far. It only took me about 10 minutes to figure out what was going on, and maybe 10 more to lock in. The concept of Va Savoir is very simple: what if we took a classic, tightly wound, laugh-a-minute screwball romantic comedy of the 30s and 40s and stretched it wayy out. What if we luxuriated in it. Loose, flowing, laugh-every-five-to-seven-minutes. Lively Paris in the summer: a walk along the Seine, sitting quietly in the library. Let's stay and watch this play-within-a-movie for a little bit. I made us a cake. This is all to say, it was a thoroughly relaxing movie that was just so easy to hang out in. In accord with the pace of the work, the plot too deals in simple, overlapping mysteries. A long lost 18th century manuscript..a possessive ex lover...a missing diamond ring...good clean fun! I could barely contain myself as they wrapped things up in the last 15 minutes. When Camille gives Ugo the diamond ring and he triumphantly announces their failing theatre troupe is saved I reflexively burst into applause. Such an outpouring of resonant human relationships, of understanding and misunderstanding. Generous filmmaking, offering a peak into what stirs in and around us..yupp. I had the biggest dumbest smile on my face as we stepped out of the theater. The mood in the car on the way home was electric. There is a 220 minute directors cut of Va Savoir, I think we (yes, you and I) should save it for a day about 6 months from now, when the high temperature reaches a disappointing 63 or so, and we're in the mood to try and squeeze out just a little more summer.

Haut, Bas, Fragile (4/4): Hot on the heels of Va Savoir, Loren and I needed more Rivette ASAP. Joined by Ira, we sat down on my couch for a late morning matinee of Haut, Bas, Fragile, Jacques' take on the magical big budget studio musicals of the 50s. A recipe for some serious Hellie catnip, and it mostly was! All the patient abundance of Va Savoir, punctuated by ecstatic (somehow naturalistic?) song and dance numbers. When the guy starts welding in the background of the Fred and Ginger style slow dance I started screaming and punching the air. Even outside the musical numbers, the dialogue is lyrical and sharp, full of jokes and rhymes. Really a joy to witness. Unfortunately, the story wasn't quite as intricately constructed, and it felt like it was struggling to find its way at times (one of the plots never even overlaps with the rest of the movie at all! Missed opportunity to say the least, I almost wish it wasn't there). I think, were I watching it in an actual theater, or in a particularly lazy mood, I would have been much more willing to forgive this. As it stands, I remedied it by meeting its laissez faire attitude with my own. I spent the middle third wandering in and out of the living room while I cooked some lunch. Felt great, and I didn't miss a thing of consequence (because, and I say this with great affection, there is nothing of consequence in this movie). Anyway, certainly didn't scratch the itch the same way Savoir did, but I have been revisiting the dance numbers all week, and they hit every time.

Reds (4/6): For reasons totally lost to time, Loren and I had been hyping up this screening for months. When the CFS calendar dropped in like, early February, we were immediately buzzing. I was telling everyone I know "You gottaa come to this show. 3 hour historical epic about communism and true love. Event of the season". How wrong I was. Forgettable, interminable picture. Warren Beatty's aw shucks jackass polyamory fuckup routine wears thin about 20 minutes into the 195 runtime. Dianne Keaton is of course, beautiful and extremely swagged out, though her character is kind of confusing and contradictory and never really gives us anything to chew on. The look Loren and I gave each other as the lights came up for intermission..not a chance we would be staying for part 2. We both, along with Annie Belle, ended up bailing. Despite not enjoying the movie, nor having any strong feelings about it, the act of leaving did stir me towards reflection. The serenity I felt in the 12 hour period after I walked out of Reds was unmatched. I usually sleep a bit restlessly in Annie Belle's bed (or any bed that's not my own, really), but I was out like a light and woke up feeling like a newborn baby. Biking home on a 30 degree morning wearing 4 sweaters and no coat should have kind of sucked, but I could summon nothing but a genuine "Yes! This is how I want to spend my time" as I followed the river northwards. There is so much power in the decisive rebuke. I know what I want!
