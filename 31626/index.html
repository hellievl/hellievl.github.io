The Gleaners and I, Apparitions

A few nights ago I threw on Agnes Varda's The Gleaners and I (I do not think the English title does the playful Les Glaneurs et la Glaneuse justice). The last time I watched this movie was around 4 years ago. I don't recall it having much of an impact on me at the time, I can't even remember where I watched it or who with (likely with Ariella at a certain vegetarian co op). I was mostly just looking for something sweet to wind down with before bed, but it ended up knocking me absolutely senseless.

I would not say, on any fundamental level, that I was a different person when I last watched this movie. Nothing about 26 year old Hellie would shock 22 year old Hellie. But I do think that I have had a number of experiences that have encouraged me to focus in, give myself over, and redouble my efforts in the interim. I was still saying things like "I don't watch movies" despite the fact that I had been watching a movie with Ira every night for months. I had yet to encounter the pleasure of pulling a carrot straight out of the ground, wiping (some) of the dirt off on your thigh, and eating it right then and there. I had not yet professionally managed a vegetable harvest or gone gleaning myself. Lest this blog post become an inventory of everything that's happened to me since then, I will say that the important part is, I hadn't yet thought seriously about what it means to look closely at something.

Gleaners looks like a movie I usually wouldn't get very excited about. At first glance, the subject matter is a bit scattershot. Conceptual threads are picked up by the handful (and occasionally dropped with little fanfare). Varda wanders from scene to scene, driving through France, interviewing whoever happens to be hanging around, and poking at whatever interests her with her nifty digital camcorder. To borrow Annie Belle's phrasing, the formal beats follow those of a skate video. It's easy to dismiss it as a kind of luxurious vlog. If you're paying any attention at all though, it takes a beautiful and astonishingly comprehensive shape. It is a movie about the rich rewards of watching.

There is a passage in Willa Cather's Death Comes for the Archbishop that will not leave my mind:

"Father Vaillant began pacing restlessly up and down as he spoke, and the Bishop watched him, musing. It was just this in his friend that was dear to him. "Where there is great love there are always miracles," he said at length. "One might almost say that an apparition is human vision corrected by divine love. I do not see you as you really are, Joseph; I see you through my affection for you. The miracles of the church seem to me to rest not so much upon faces or voices or healing power coming suddenly near to us from afar off, but upon our perceptions being made finer, so that for a moment our eyes can see and our ears can hear what is there about us always.""

Varda, through incredible focus (meditation even!), corrects her vision, our vision. We are presented with a series of apparitions, of miracles composed of what is there about us always.

Rooting through a field of potatoes. They're absolutely free, they're going to taste amazing, and there are quite literally more than enough for everyone who wants some.

Sunlight shining on your aging hand through a car windshield, years of joy and indescribable pain and increase and delicious movement, it will all continue tomorrow, wow.

They've made a map that shows you where to find everything you need to furnish a home, a friend's home, a neighbor's home, they didn't really make it for that, but you can use it like that.

I have always considered the camera to be the ideal tool for this kind of work. If you've talked to me at all in the past few months, I've probably read you the Doug Ischar quote about photography's knack for showing us "the moment in which interactions between people, between people and objects, between people and a precariously occupied environment achieve their most resonant relationships" Laid bare, those relationships appear to us as miracles (and they are, to be clear). Photography, still or moving, is revelation, not construction. Actually I think this is something 22 year old Hellie might disagree on. Having recently finished a long series of deeply necessary, but artistically worthless self portraits, she would probably find the idea annoying and lightly scandalous, or at least useless for her purposes.

Anyway, I was feeling moved enough by all this to finally pony up and order a camcorder like I've been threatening to do. Could home movies......be the......well I won't say it.

Holy coincidence, invisible thread, thank you for all that you allow me to see.
